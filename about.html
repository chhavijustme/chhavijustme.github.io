Project Brief

We designed a interactive audio-visual educational module to educate the ASHA workers about first aid after snake bite, following a rigorous design methodology. The project was done as part of the course “Introduction of Interaction Design”.



Who are ASHA workers?

Accredited social health activists (ASHAs) is community health workers instituted by the government of India. The plan is to have "an ASHA in every village”, and a total of 870,089 ASHA workers were reported in July 2013 in India.



Need of the Project

The training modules for an ASHA worker in India prove to be ineffective due to the non- engaging methodology followed. They neither provide an interactive feedback based learning nor do they prove to be ready reference in case of an emergency.

India has been long known as a country of snake charmers by the western population, though the high death count due to snake bites suggest otherwise. The count can be accounted to wide species variation, shortage of anti-snake venom (ASV), poor compliance with treatment protocols, lack of public education and clear policy to deal with the problem.

The community health workers, especially ASHA, who mostly function as the first point of contact in case of a medical emergency play a major role in first aid treatment. 70% of all snakebites are from nonvenomous species and only 50% of bites by venomous species actually envenomate the patient.

Effective administration of first aid by an ASHA can go a long way in saving a life.



Secondary Research

There are 6 official booklets by the Health Ministry of India for training and induction of ASHA members. We did a thorough analysis of the training material, the government policies and programmes Along with that, design interventions across the globe in community health were studied for reference.



Primary Research

Field studies were conducted in Health Sub- Center, Amingaon, Guwahati and the Primary Health Center, Guwahati. One on one interviews with four ASHA members and field worker were conducted. A focus group study was also conducted to further gain insights. It was challenging because of the language barrier (their native language is Assamese, which I am not familiar with) and also because of their disinterest in sharing as they are very busy.



Affinity Analysis

We coded the interviews into snippets and divided them to discover the major themes. The irregular training sessions, non-engaging modules and lack of ready reference were the primary themes. The lack of technical know how and the varying literacy levels of ASHA workers were the secondary themes.
